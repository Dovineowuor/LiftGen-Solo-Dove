
# Technical Whitepaper

## Distributed Micro-Energy Harvesting in Vertical Transportation Systems

## Abstract

This paper presents the engineering design and theoretical framework for **LiftGen**, a retrofit energy-harvesting and telemetry platform designed for elevator infrastructure. The system employs piezoelectric transducers and micro-electromagnetic generators to capture mechanical vibrations, structural oscillations, and friction-induced motion present in normal elevator operation. Harvested energy is conditioned and stored using a hybrid architecture combining high-cycle supercapacitors and lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO₄) batteries.

The LiftGen design prioritizes strict **non-interference with operational safety systems**, electrical isolation from elevator control circuits, and modular installation compatible with existing vertical transportation infrastructure. In addition to auxiliary energy production, the platform integrates distributed sensing and telemetry capabilities enabling predictive maintenance through vibration spectral analysis and operational monitoring.

The proposed architecture demonstrates that elevator systems—traditionally viewed only as energy consumers—can also function as distributed micro-energy sources contributing to building monitoring infrastructure and low-power auxiliary systems.

# 1. Introduction

Elevators are essential components of modern urban architecture, enabling vertical mobility in residential, commercial, and industrial buildings. Contemporary elevator designs emphasize passenger safety, ride quality, and energy efficiency, with improvements such as regenerative drives, optimized counterweight balancing, and advanced motor control systems.

Despite these advancements, a considerable portion of mechanical energy generated during elevator operation remains **unutilized**. Energy is dissipated through structural vibrations, friction between guide shoes and rails, acoustic resonances within the shaft, and transient compressive forces during motion transitions. While individually small, these energy events occur continuously throughout elevator operation.

LiftGen addresses this inefficiency by introducing a **distributed micro-energy harvesting network** embedded within the elevator environment. Rather than capturing energy directly from the primary drive system, LiftGen focuses on harvesting **secondary mechanical phenomena** that are typically ignored in conventional designs.

This distributed approach offers several advantages:

The system operates independently of the elevator drive and safety chain.
Installation can be performed as a retrofit with minimal mechanical modification.
Harvested energy can power telemetry infrastructure and auxiliary electronics.
Structural vibration data can be used for predictive maintenance analytics.

By combining energy harvesting with sensor telemetry, LiftGen transforms elevator infrastructure into a **dual-purpose system** capable of generating both electrical power and operational intelligence.

# 2. Energy Harvesting Mechanisms

LiftGen employs multiple harvesting technologies distributed throughout the elevator structure. Each node is designed to capture a specific mechanical energy phenomenon present during elevator operation.

## 2.1 Piezoelectric Vibration Harvesting

Piezoelectric transducers are installed on structural members of the cabin frame and floor assembly. These components experience continuous micro-vibrations generated by guide rail interaction, passenger load variations, and motor acceleration events.

Piezoelectric materials exhibit the **direct piezoelectric effect**, whereby mechanical stress induces an electric potential across the crystal lattice. When subjected to oscillatory forces, these devices generate alternating electrical signals proportional to the amplitude and frequency of the vibration.

Elevator cabin structures typically exhibit vibration frequencies within the range of 5–120 Hz depending on system design and load conditions. Piezoelectric harvesters tuned to these frequencies can capture structural vibration energy and convert it into high-voltage, low-current electrical signals suitable for rectification and storage.

These sensors simultaneously function as vibration monitoring devices, enabling spectral analysis of cabin dynamics and early detection of abnormal mechanical behavior.

---

## 2.2 Micro-Linear Electromagnetic Generators

Micro-linear generators are integrated into guide shoe assemblies and rail interface points. These devices operate on electromagnetic induction principles similar to miniature linear alternators.

During elevator travel, slight lateral movements occur between the guide shoes and rails due to structural tolerances, rail alignment variations, and dynamic load conditions. Although these displacements are extremely small, the repetitive motion provides an opportunity for kinetic energy harvesting.

The generator contains a permanent magnet assembly moving relative to a stationary coil winding. As relative displacement occurs, the changing magnetic field induces a current within the coil according to Faraday’s law of electromagnetic induction.

The harvested power levels are typically modest, but the continuous nature of elevator travel results in cumulative energy capture over time. Because these generators are mechanically coupled to guide shoe assemblies, they also provide valuable data regarding rail alignment and friction characteristics.

---

## 2.3 Counterweight Motion Harvesting

In traction elevator systems, the counterweight travels opposite the cabin, balancing a significant portion of the load. This motion generates oscillations in the suspension system and small vibrational forces within the shaft structure.

LiftGen counterweight nodes utilize vibration harvesters and electromagnetic micro-generators mounted on counterweight frames or guide assemblies. These harvesters capture mechanical energy associated with vertical motion, acceleration transitions, and counterweight guide interactions.

Because counterweights move during nearly every elevator trip, these nodes represent a consistent energy source across typical building usage cycles.

---

## 2.4 Buffer Region Harvesters

The elevator pit contains buffer mechanisms designed to absorb energy in the event of over-travel or during maintenance testing procedures. Although such events occur infrequently, the pit environment experiences structural vibrations transmitted through the shaft structure during normal operation.

Buffer region harvesters capture this vibrational energy and convert it into stored power that can support **pit-level sensors**, environmental monitoring devices, and safety inspection systems.

These nodes may also be used to power low-maintenance monitoring devices such as water intrusion detectors, temperature sensors, and shaft condition monitors.

# 3. Power Conditioning and Storage

Energy generated by harvesting nodes is inherently irregular, characterized by variable voltage levels, fluctuating frequencies, and intermittent pulses. Efficient power conditioning is therefore critical to maximize energy recovery and maintain stable storage behavior.

## 3.1 AC–DC Conversion and Impedance Matching

Each harvesting node includes a dedicated power conditioning circuit consisting of a bridge rectifier, impedance-matching network, and input buffer capacitor.

The rectifier converts the alternating signals produced by piezoelectric or electromagnetic generators into direct current suitable for storage. Because harvesting devices typically exhibit high output impedance, the impedance matching network ensures optimal power transfer from the generator to the storage circuit.

The buffer capacitor smooths transient pulses and reduces voltage fluctuations before energy is transferred to the central energy aggregation hub.

---

## 3.2 Hybrid Energy Storage Architecture

LiftGen employs a two-stage energy storage architecture designed to accommodate both short-duration energy bursts and long-term storage requirements.

### Supercapacitor Buffer Bank

Supercapacitors serve as the primary energy buffer within the system. These devices exhibit extremely high charge and discharge rates, allowing them to capture rapid energy pulses produced during elevator motion events such as acceleration, deceleration, and vibration spikes.

Supercapacitors also provide long operational lifetimes with cycle counts exceeding one million charge cycles, making them well suited for high-frequency energy harvesting environments.

### LiFePO₄ Battery Storage

Energy accumulated within the supercapacitor bank is gradually transferred to a lithium iron phosphate battery pack through a dedicated charge controller. LiFePO₄ chemistry is selected due to its thermal stability, long cycle life, and improved safety characteristics compared to conventional lithium-ion batteries.

The battery bank functions as the primary energy reservoir supporting telemetry systems, distributed sensors, and auxiliary building electronics.

# 4. Safety and Regulatory Compliance

Elevator systems operate within one of the most strictly regulated engineering environments. Safety standards such as the **EN 81 series** and related international codes define strict requirements for system reliability and fail-safe operation.

LiftGen is designed explicitly as a **non-critical auxiliary system** to ensure full compliance with these regulatory frameworks.

### Electrical Isolation

All LiftGen electrical subsystems are isolated from the elevator control system using opto-isolators, galvanic isolation transformers, and air-gap separation techniques. This prevents any possibility of electrical interference with safety circuits or control logic.

### Non-Interference Architecture

The system does not interface with critical elevator components including door interlocks, overspeed governors, braking systems, or main drive controllers. Mechanical coupling is limited to passive mounting points that do not alter the structural or operational characteristics of the elevator.

### Fail-Safe Behavior

LiftGen is designed with a fully passive failure mode. If the system experiences a fault or becomes disconnected, elevator operation continues unaffected. No operational dependency exists between LiftGen and the elevator drive or safety systems.

This design philosophy ensures that the introduction of energy harvesting capabilities does not compromise the fundamental safety principles of vertical transportation systems.

# 5. Telemetry and Predictive Maintenance

Beyond energy harvesting, LiftGen provides a distributed sensing platform capable of monitoring elevator dynamics in real time. Each harvesting node incorporates sensing capabilities that capture mechanical vibration data and energy generation metrics.

The telemetry gateway aggregates this information and performs preliminary processing before transmitting data to building management systems or remote monitoring platforms.

Key telemetry metrics include vibration spectral density through FFT analysis, cumulative harvested energy expressed in watt-hours, and storage system health indicators including battery state of charge and state of health.

These measurements provide valuable insight into the mechanical condition of elevator components. For example, increasing vibration amplitudes in specific frequency bands may indicate early wear in guide shoes, rail alignment deviations, or motor bearing degradation.

By detecting such patterns before they evolve into operational failures, LiftGen enables predictive maintenance strategies that reduce downtime and improve elevator reliability.

# 6. System Scalability and Building Integration

Although LiftGen operates at relatively low power levels compared to primary building energy systems, its distributed architecture enables scalable deployment across multiple elevators within a building.

In large commercial structures containing several elevator shafts, the aggregated energy harvested from multiple LiftGen installations can support building monitoring infrastructure, wireless sensor networks, and low-power building automation systems.

Future implementations may also integrate LiftGen data streams with smart building platforms, enabling deeper insights into elevator usage patterns, structural behavior, and long-term maintenance trends.

LiftGen demonstrates that elevator infrastructure can be transformed into a source of distributed micro-energy while simultaneously enhancing system monitoring capabilities. The combination of hybrid energy storage, distributed sensing, and predictive telemetry creates a platform that contributes both to building energy optimization and to the long-term reliability of vertical transportation systems.

As buildings become increasingly intelligent and interconnected, systems such as LiftGen illustrate how existing infrastructure can be augmented to provide new functionality, improved efficiency, and deeper operational insight.

# 8. Mathematical Power Yield Modeling

The power harvesting potential of LiftGen ($P_{harv}$) is a function of the available ambient kinetic and vibrational energy ($E_k$) and the conversion efficiency ($\eta$) of the transducer array.

### 8.1 Vibration Harvesting Equations
For a piezoelectric cantilever node, the instantaneous power $P(t)$ is modeled by:
$$P(t) = \frac{k^2 \cdot Q^2 \cdot \omega^3 \cdot Y_0^2 \cdot \zeta}{\omega_n^2}$$
Where:
- $k$: Electromechanical coupling coefficient.
- $Q$: Quality factor of the resonator.
- $\omega$: Operational frequency (vibration).
- $Y_0$: Vibration amplitude (displacement).
- $\xi$: Damping ratio.

### 8.2 Ride-Cycle Estimation
For a standard 10-floor ride (30m travel), the available kinetic energy from guide rail friction $F_f$ can be estimated:
$$W_f = \int F_f \, ds \approx \mu \cdot m \cdot g \cdot D$$
The system targets a capture of $0.05\%$ of this energy. For a 1000kg cabin, $W_f \approx 29,430 \, \text{Joules}$. A $0.05\%$ harvest yields **14.7 Joules per cycle**, sufficient to power low-energy telemetry nodes indefinitely.

# 9. Experimental Prototype Methodology

To validate the theoretical yields, the following field testing protocol is established:

### 9.1 Instrumented Test Shaft
- **Deployment**: Install 4 nodes on a 1500kg traction lift.
- **Placement**: Counterweight (N1), Main Cabin Frame (N2, N3), Guide Shoe (N4).
- **Control**: Reference power meter installed on the main lift VFD DC bus to monitor baseline draw.

### 9.2 Data Collection Protocol
1. **Calibration Run**: Empty cabin travel to establish structural "noise" harmonics.
2. **Payload Sweep**: Incremental testing with 0%, 50%, and 100% rated load.
3. **Harmonic Mapping**: Recording vibration spectral density using the node's internal FFT engine (10Hz–500Hz).

### 9.3 Validation Metrics
- **Specific Yield**: Watts harvested per meter of travel (W/m).
- **SOC Delta**: Battery State-of-Charge gain per 100 cycles.
- **Harmonic Correlation**: Correlation between elevator health scoring and harvested spectral jitter.
